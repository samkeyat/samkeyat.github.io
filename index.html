About Me

Who I Am

I am an atmospheric scientist with a deep interest in the physical processes that govern extreme weather. My career has taken me across three continents — from Bangladesh, where I grew up, to Germany, where I trained in environmental physics and remote sensing, to the United States, where I earned my Ph.D. and now work at NCAR.

My doctoral work at the University of Arizona focused on improving convective-permitting model simulations of extreme precipitation through object-based tracking and data assimilation. As a postdoc at NCAR's Research Applications Laboratory, I continue this work with an emphasis on operational forecasting and machine learning.

Beyond science, I'm a poet and a cricket all-rounder — I believe curiosity, whether on the field or in the atmosphere, is the best compass.

Research

What I Work On

My work sits at the intersection of numerical modeling, observations, and data-driven methods, with a focus on understanding and predicting high-impact weather events.

Numerical Weather Forecasting

Convective-permitting simulations using WRF and CESM over complex terrain.

Data Assimilation

Integrating GPS precipitable water vapor and multi-sensor observations to improve model accuracy.

MCS Tracking

Object-based tracking of mesoscale convective systems linked to extreme precipitation events.

Machine Learning

Applying ML techniques to weather forecasting, storm classification, and climate variability.

North American Monsoon

Studying convective dynamics and precipitation predictability using observing system simulation experiments (OSSEs).

Remote Sensing

Satellite image analysis, DOAS, FTIR spectroscopy, and cloud radiative forcing from GOME-2 measurements.
